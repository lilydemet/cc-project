A MANIFESTO for mapping negative space


a Manifesto is a declaration.

you navigated to this page and are reading these words because I declared the document in which they are written to be html.

the Manifesto of this Manifesto is: <DOCTYPE html> (right click here and choose "inspect" to see what i mean).

a document's declaration allows a web browser (like google chrome, apple safari, or mozilla firefox) to read its contents and display it according to the rules of its language. if not for <DOCTYPE html>, what you are reading would not exist here.

just as a document declaration makes my html file legible to a web browser, my Manifesto For Mapping Negative Space provides me, the web (and urban) flâneur, a language with which to communicate what it is i do. it articulates a framework which brings form to an otherwise indefinite concept.

in what follows I provide directive, though no direction. wander and wonder as you will. go forth and glean. that's all im really doing.



a sticker on a lamppost in gastown offers the first directive for mapping negative space: AVOID THE SUBJECT.

negative space is a concept that, in art, refers to that which surrounds the subject––space that is necessary in order for the subject to exist but which is not the intended focus of attention.
in writing, negative space is whitespace. its all that the words are not. its in the margins and the distance between a period and capital letter. creative writing and poetry in particular engage (with) the page in ways that incorperate space into the creative work itself. the space left empty carries value beyond legibility. it gives room for the reader to sit with what words have evoked. creative forms of writing are well suited to render encounters of deep mapping as they allow for juxtaposition and the dissolution of space/time linearity. Anachronisms and anachorisms allow multiple realities to be woven together.


TAKE YOUR TIME
Day to day we use maps to find our way about. We use digital maps to find out when the next bus is coming and how long it will take me to get where we are to where we are going. But if there were no destination, what would the journey be like? Mapping negative space requires slowing down in order to be with the landscape rather than merely moving through it. By taking my time, i am practicing Slow Scholarship. my project is just the beggining––a proof of concept, where concept is inchoate and proof subjective.

BUILD FOR THE LOVELY UNEXPECTED this directive i take from The short instructional manifesto for relationship anarchy, by Andie Nordgren. Within the framework of relationshiip anarchy, to build for the lovely unexpected is to be open... open to the unexpected, the unplanned. it is to express oneself freely. in my counter cartographic practice of deep mapping, building for the lovely unexpected means centering exploration, opening myself to the serendipitous and, above all else, "being free to be spontaneous".


Mapping Negative Space is a cartography of in-betweens––spaces, places, sensations, lifeworlds, ways of being, and processes (rhythms?) that shape conventionally intended objects of focus though are seldom mapped in their own right. Because deep mapping is a counter cartographic praxis rather than a kind of counter map, what i have here digitized is an assemblage of articulations, a spatial bricolage of my gleanings.

my effort is inspired by the Humanities special issue Spatial Bricolage: Methodological Eclecticism and the Poetics of “Making Do”. Les Roberts' editorial (and writings elsewhere on deep mapping, spatial anthroplogy, and covert ethnography) in particular introduced me to a viable way of being a bricoleur in academia.
